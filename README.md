# Introduction

This report analyzes a comprehensive dataset from the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) detailing agricultural greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. The data focuses on two primary gases—Methane (CH₄) and Carbon Dioxide (CO₂)—released from agricultural land across nearly every country from 1990 to 2021. The analysis aims to identify the world's largest agricultural emitters, track global emission trends over the past three decades, determine the relative contribution of CH₄ versus CO₂, and highlight regional patterns of increasing or decreasing emissions. The insights are critical for policymakers, researchers, and environmental stakeholders to prioritize mitigation efforts.

# Analysis Methods

The analysis was performed using Excel's data analysis capabilities, employing the following methods:

Data Aggregation & PivotTables: PivotTables were used to sum emission values (in kilotons, kt) by country, year, and gas type (CH₄ vs. CO₂). This allowed for efficient summarization of the extensive dataset.

Temporal Trend Analysis: Emissions per year were aggregated globally and for specific regions/countries. Line charts were generated to visualize changes over the 32-year period (1990-2021) and calculate absolute and percentage changes.

Comparative Analysis (Gas Proportion): The total emissions for each gas were summed across all years and countries to calculate the overall proportion of CH₄ and CO₂ in agricultural emissions.

Regional Classification: Countries were manually grouped into broad geographic regions (e.g., Asia, South America, Africa, Europe, North America) to identify macro-level trends.

# Key Findings & Interpretation

Research Question 1: Which countries are the largest emitters of agricultural GHGs?

The data clearly identifies the following as the largest and most persistent emitters of agricultural GHGs (based on cumulative CH₄ and CO₂ emissions):

Brazil: Is the single largest emitter, driven by extremely high CO₂ emissions from agricultural land (e.g., 1,396,372 kt in 1990) alongside very high CH₄ emissions (e.g., 14,125 kt in 2021). Brazil’s emissions are unique in their massive CO₂ component, likely linked to land-use change and biomass burning.

China: A top emitter, primarily due to very high CH₄ emissions (e.g., 13,703 kt in 2021). China's CO₂ emissions are significant but considerably lower than Brazil's.

India: Consistently one of the top emitters, driven predominantly by very high CH₄ emissions (e.g., 20,608 kt in 2021), which surpass those of China in recent years.

United States: A major emitter, with a balanced profile of significant CH₄ (e.g., 7,984 kt in 2021) and substantial CO₂ emissions (e.g., 80,000-90,000 kt annually in the 1990s, decreasing in later years).

Indonesia: Another top emitter with significant CH₄ releases (e.g., 8,438 kt in 2014), which show high inter-annual variability.

Research Question 2: How have global agricultural emissions changed over the past 30 years?

Global emissions show a clear and consistent upward trend. While there is year-to-year variability, the overall trajectory from 1990 to 2021 is a significant increase in total agricultural GHGs. This increase is driven primarily by rising CH₄ emissions from developing nations, which outpace reductions or stabilization in developed countries.

Research Question 3: What is the proportion of CH₄ vs CO₂ in agricultural emissions?

The analysis reveals that Methane (CH₄) is the dominant agricultural GHG in the dataset, accounting for the majority of emissions when considering the data as presented (raw kilotons). This is due to the widespread and large-volume sources of CH₄, such as enteric fermentation (livestock) and rice cultivation. While CO₂ emissions are extremely high for certain countries (notably Brazil), CH₄ is the more consistent and globally pervasive gas in agricultural systems.

Research Question 4: Which regions show increasing vs decreasing emission trends?

Increasing Emission Trends (Significantly):

South Asia (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh): Shows the most dramatic and consistent increase, especially in CH₄ emissions, driven by agricultural intensification to feed growing populations.

Sub-Saharan Africa (Nigeria, Ethiopia, Tanzania, Chad): Displays a strong and persistent increasing trend across many countries, reflecting the expansion of agricultural land and livestock production.

South America (excluding Brazil): Countries like Argentina, Bolivia, and Paraguay show notable increases in both CH₄ and CO₂, linked to agricultural expansion.

Decreasing or Stabilizing Emission Trends:

Europe (EU countries, e.g., Germany, France, Poland, UK): Most European nations show a clear decreasing trend in agricultural emissions, particularly since the 1990s. This is a result of agricultural policies, improved efficiency, and reduced livestock numbers.

North America (USA, Canada): Emissions have largely stabilized and show a slight decreasing trend from peak levels in the 1990s and 2000s, due to efficiency gains and changes in agricultural practices.

Russia & Former Soviet States: Show a notable decreasing trend following the economic restructuring of the 1990s.

# Conclusions

Agricultural emissions are not static. While developed nations in Europe and North America have successfully reduced or stabilized their agricultural GHG footprints, this progress is being overshadowed by rapid and sustained increases in populous developing regions of Asia, Africa, and South America.

Methane is the core challenge. As the dominant agricultural GHG, effective mitigation must prioritize reducing CH₄ from livestock (e.g., through feed additives, improved manure management) and rice paddies (e.g., alternative water management).

Land-use related CO₂ is a critical "hotspot." The outsized impact of CO₂ emissions from countries like Brazil highlights that agricultural emissions are not just about livestock and rice. Managing biomass burning and land-use change is essential for major emitters.

Global progress is unbalanced. The overall trend is upward, meaning current global efforts are insufficient to reverse the rise in agricultural emissions. Targeted international support and technology transfer to high-growth regions are urgently needed.

# Recommendations

Based on this analysis, the following recommendations are made:

Prioritize Mitigation in High-Growth Regions: International climate finance and technical cooperation should be strategically directed towards South Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa to help these nations adopt low-emission agricultural intensification methods before their emissions lock in at high levels.

Invest in Methane Reduction Technologies: Increase research, development, and deployment of cost-effective solutions for reducing enteric methane (e.g., seaweed-based feed additives, breeding low-methane livestock) and methane from rice cultivation (e.g., Alternate Wetting and Drying - AWD).

Address Land-Use CO₂: For major emitters like Brazil and other nations in the Amazon basin, policies and incentives must be strengthened to reduce deforestation and associated biomass burning, which constitute a massive and avoidable source of agricultural CO₂.

Maintain and Replicate European Success: Analyze the specific policies and practices that led to the successful decline of agricultural emissions in Europe. Where applicable, these models should be adapted and promoted in other regions with stabilizing or slowly decreasing emissions, such as North America.
